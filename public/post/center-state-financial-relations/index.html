1. Vertical AND Horizontal Fiscal

Vertical Fiscal Imbalance: “Money vs. Duty” Gap

  • Center: Levies elastic, broad-based taxes (Corporate Tax, Income Tax, Customs Duties) → ensures uniformity, prevents market distortion, maintains macroeconomic stability
  • States: Bear high-expenditure duties (Police, Public Health, Agriculture, local infrastructure) but depend on limited, inelastic sources (stamp duty, alcohol excise)
  • Result: Center’s revenue surplus vs. States’ revenue deficit → vertical gap → mandates top-down resource flow

Horizontal Fiscal Imbalance: “Rich vs. Poor State” Gap

  • Advantaged states (Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat): ports, industrialization, large corporate tax base
  • Disadvantaged states (Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand): historical deficits, difficult terrain, high population, low per-capita income
  • Result: Without redistribution → unequal public service access → horizontal gap → mandates cross-state redistribution for baseline citizen services

[!summary] Core Distinction Vertical = Revenue mismatch between Center & States (function vs. finance). Horizontal = Equity gap between States (rich vs. poor). Both justify Finance Commission devolution.

[!question]- Active Recall: Vertical Imbalance ? Why are elastic taxes assigned to Center? → Prevent market fragmentation, ensure national uniformity ? Name 2 State expenditure responsibilities → Police, Public Health (or Agriculture, local infrastructure) ? Name 2 inelastic State tax sources → Stamp duty, Alcohol excise

[!question]- Active Recall: Horizontal Imbalance ? Name 3 advantaged states + 1 reason each → Maharashtra (ports), Tamil Nadu (industrialization), Gujarat (corporate base) ? Name 3 disadvantages of Bihar/UP/Jharkhand → Historical deficits, difficult terrain, low per-capita income ? What baseline goal justifies horizontal redistribution? → Equal public service access for every Indian citizen, regardless of geography

  • The Finance Commission is the engineer hired every 5 years to calculate exactly how to fix these two imbalances.

  • Tax Sharing and Grants-in-Aid are the pipes used to move the money.

Tax Sharing vs. Grants-in-Aid: Quick Comparison

Feature Tax Sharing (The “Right”) Grants-in-Aid (The “Need”)
Purpose Fix Vertical Imbalance Fix Horizontal Imbalance + emergencies
Taxes Covered Income Tax, Corporate Tax (net proceeds) Consolidated Fund of India allocations
Nature Unconditional; States decide spending Often conditional; Center specifies use
Determination Finance Commission recommends share (e.g., 41% to States) Finance Commission recommends need-based amounts
Flexibility Formula-based (population, area, etc.) Adapts to localized needs (disasters, tribal welfare)
Example Use Salaries, roads, general welfare Odisha cyclone relief, Assam ST welfare, primary health centers

2. Art 268 → 271

Verb Meaning Exam Trap Alert
Levied Who passes law & imposes tax? Center levies all in this bucket
Collected Whose machinery physically collects? States collect only in Art 268
Appropriated/Assigned/Distributed Who keeps & spends proceeds? Key differentiator across articles

[!tip] Gradient Mnemonic Art 268 → 271: Benefit shifts State-favorable → Center-favorable
268 (States keep 100%) → 269 (States get assigned) → 270 (Shared pool) → 271 (Center keeps 100% surcharge)

Article Who Levies? (Makes the Law) Who Collects? Who Keeps the Money? Best Example to Remember UPSC Implication / Friction Point
268 Center States States Stamp duties on Bills of Exchange Center sets uniform rates for national ease of business, but states get the revenue.
269 Center Center States Taxes on Inter-State trade (excluding GST) Center must step in because one state cannot tax another’s borders.
269A Center Center Shared (via GST Council) IGST (Integrated GST) The backbone of the GST architecture. Solves the “tax on tax” cascading effect.
270 Center Center Shared (Divisible Pool) Income Tax, Corporate Tax The Core of Fiscal Federalism. The Finance Commission (Art 280) decides the split.
271 Center Center Center (Exclusive) Surcharges & Cesses (e.g., Krishi Kalyan Cess) Major Current Affairs Friction. States get nothing from this. The Center is increasingly using cesses to deny states their share.

Article 271: Surcharge & Cess Controversy

  • Article 270: Mandates Centre share tax revenue with states (current devolution: 41%)
  • Article 271: Cesses/Surcharges excluded from divisible pool; Centre retains fully
  • State grievance: Rising reliance on Health & Education Cess, super-rich surcharges → shrinks divisible pool % of gross tax revenue
  • Federalism angle: States contend this undermines cooperative federalism spirit ( #📝)

Article 268A Deletion :

Service Tax #Evaluation (1994-2017): Timeline & Prelims Facts

  • 1994 Origin: Dr. Raja Chelliah Committee recommendation → Introduced by Dr. Manmohan Singh (Finance Minister) in 1994 Budget
  • Initial scope: Positive List approach; taxed only 3 services (Telephones, Non-life Insurance, Stockbrokers) at 5%
  • Constitutional basis: Centre used Entry 97, Union List (Residuary Powers) as “services” absent from 7th Schedule

[!summary] 88th Amendment Act, 2003 (High-Yield Prelims)

  • Inserted Entry 92C in Union List: Explicit Centre power to tax services
  • Inserted Article 268A: Service Tax levied by Centre, collected & appropriated by Centre + States

Structural Shift: Positive → Negative List (2012)

  • Trigger: Services sector >50% of GDP; taxing via Positive List became unmanageable
  • New rule: All services taxable by default EXCEPT essential services on Negative List (public transport, primary healthcare, schooling)
  • Impact: Dramatically expanded tax base with minimal legislative overhead

Rate Escalation & Cesses :

  • Base rate: 14%
    • Swachh Bharat Cess (0.5%) + Krishi Kalyan Cess (0.5%)
  • Effective rate: 15% (Prelims data point)

Termination & Constitutional Cleanup (2017)

  • 101st Constitutional Amendment Act (GST): Service Tax subsumed into GST w.e.f. July 1, 2017
  • Deleted provisions: Article 268A + Entry 92C (rendered obsolete post-GST)

[!hint]- Active Recall: Constitutional Entries Which entries empowered Service Tax pre-GST, and what happened to them? || Entry 97 (Residuary Powers, initial levy) → Entry 92C + Article 268A (88th Amendment, 2003) → Both deleted by 101st Amendment (GST, 2017).

[!hint]- Active Recall: List Approach Shift Contrast Positive List vs. Negative List in Service Tax. || Positive List (1994): Only named services taxed. Negative List (2012): All services taxable except named essentials → broader base, fewer exemptions.

[!hint]- Active Recall: Cess Impact Name the two cesses added to Service Tax and their combined effect. || Swachh Bharat Cess (0.5%) + Krishi Kalyan Cess (0.5%) → Raised effective rate from 14% to 15% (2015-16).

[!hint]- Why do states oppose increased cess/surcharge usage? || Because Article 271 excludes them from divisible pool, reducing states’ share of gross tax revenue despite same tax burden on citizens.

[!hint]- Which amendment deleted Article 268A and why? || 101st Amendment (GST, 2016); Service Tax subsumed into GST, making Art 268A obsolete.

3. 📊 GRANTS-IN-AID TO STATES: Conceptual Framework

[!summary] 💡 Metaphor for Quick Recall Art 275 = Monthly allowance (scheduled, right-based, formula-driven)
Art 282 = Uncle’s gift (occasional, discretionary, purpose-specific)

1️⃣ ARTICLE 275: STATUTORY GRANTS

🔑 Key Features

  • Nature: Parliamentary grants to states in need of financial assistance (not all states)
  • Authority: Based on Finance Commission recommendations (Centre cannot play favorites)
  • Purpose:
    • Cover revenue deficits remaining after tax sharing
    • Promote welfare of STs in states
    • Raise administration level of Scheduled Areas (including Assam)
  • Financial Character: Charged on Consolidated Fund of India
  • Qualities: Objective, formula-driven, non-discriminatory

[!question]- Active Recall: Art 275 Q1: Which body recommends statutory grants?
Q2: Are these grants given to all states?
Q3: On which fund are they charged?
Q4: Name two specific purposes beyond general assistance.

[!success]- ANS :

A1: Finance Commission
A2: No, only states “in need of assistance”
A3: Consolidated Fund of India
A4: ST welfare; Scheduled Areas administration (including Assam)

2️⃣ ARTICLE 282: DISCRETIONARY GRANTS

🔑 Key Features

  • Nature: Discretionary grants by Centre or States
  • Scope: For any public purpose (even outside legislative domain)
  • Authority: Entirely at discretion of Union Ministers/Government
  • Role of Finance Commission: None (key differentiator from Art 275)
  • Purpose:
    • Funding specific schemes
    • Welfare programs
    • Emergency disaster relief

[!question]- Active Recall: Art 282 Q1: Can grants be given for purposes outside legislative competence?
Q2: What role does Finance Commission play?
Q3: Who has the final decision-making authority?
Q4: Give two examples of purposes.

[!success]- ANS : A1: Yes, any “public purpose”
A2: No role whatsoever
A3: Union Ministers/Government (discretionary)
A4: Specific schemes, welfare programs, disaster relief (any two)

Parameter Article 275 Article 282
Nature Statutory (right-based) Discretionary (choice-based)
Recommendation Finance Commission mandatory No FC role
Coverage Only needy states Any state
Purpose General + ST/Scheduled Areas Any public purpose
Fund Charged on CFI Not specified
Character Formula-driven, objective Political discretion

✅ Art 275 includes Assam specifically for Scheduled Areas administration
✅ Both general AND specific grants under Art 275 need FC recommendation
✅ Art 282 allows extra-domain spending (beyond legislative lists)
✅ Planning Commission historically used Art 282 (now defunct, but concept remains)

#📝 PERSPECTIVE: Art 275 vs 282 — Federalism Tension

[!summary] Framers’ Intent: Art 275 = main highway (FC-recommended grants) | Art 282 = tiny side road (rare emergencies)

📉 What Actually Happened? (1950-2014)

Planning Commission’s Role

  • Status: Non-statutory, non-constitutional body
  • Duration: ~65 years of operation
  • Mechanism: Used Art 282’s “blank check” to fund:
    • Five-Year Plans
    • Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS)
  • Control: Ruling party at Centre → political discretion

States’ Grievance

  • Art 282 grants became highly political & discriminatory

[!danger] Metaphor Alert Tiny side road (282) → 8-lane mega-highway
Constitutional highway (275) → overshadowed
Result: Spirit of Cooperative Federalism damaged

4. RESTRICTIONS ON STATE TAXING POWERS

1️⃣ PROFESSION TAX (Art. 276)

Power: States can tax professions, trades, employments, callings
Ceiling: ₹2,500 per annum (maximum limit)

2️⃣ SALES TAX RESTRICTIONS

Power: States can tax sale/purchase of goods (except newspapers)

❌ Four Constitutional Prohibitions:

  • Outside state → Cannot tax transactions outside state territory
  • Import/Export → Cannot tax sale/purchase in course of import or export
  • Interstate trade → Cannot tax sale/purchase in course of interstate trade & commerce
  • Parliament-declared goods → Cannot tax goods declared by Parliament as of special importance

[!tip] Mnemonic: O-I-I-S

[!question]- Active Recall Q1: Can states tax newspaper sales?
Q2: Name the 4 restrictions on state sales tax power.

Answer A1: No, explicitly excluded
A2: Outside state, Import/Export, Interstate trade, Parliament-declared special goods

3️⃣ ELECTRICITY TAX LIMITATIONS

Power: States can tax consumption/sale of electricity

[!failure]- Two #🚫

  • Consumed by Centre or sold to Centre
  • Railway operations → Consumed in construction, maintenance, or operation of any railway by Centre

4️⃣ INTERSTATE RIVER AUTHORITY TAXATION

Power: States can tax water/electricity stored, generated, consumed, distributed, or sold by Parliament-established authority for:

  • Regulating interstate river/river valley
  • Developing interstate river/river valley

⚠️ Critical Condition:

Presidential Assent Mandatory → Law must be reserved for President’s consideration and receive assent to be effective

[!danger] Prelims Trap This is one of few state laws requiring mandatory Presidential assent (not just Governor)

[!question]- Active Recall Q: When can a state tax interstate river authority’s water/electricity? What procedural requirement exists?

Answer When Parliament-established authority regulates/develops interstate river valley. Presidential assent is mandatory - law must be reserved for President's consideration.

#🏁

Tax Subject State Power Key Restriction
Professions/Trades ✅ Allowed Max ₹2,500/annum
Sale of Goods ✅ Allowed ❌ Newspapers, Outside state, Import/Export, Interstate, Special goods
Electricity ✅ Allowed ❌ Centre consumption/sale, Railway operations
Interstate River Authority ✅ Allowed ⚠️ Presidential assent compulsory

[!summary] Constitutional Philosophy These restrictions protect national economic unity (prevent interstate barriers) and Centre’s operational autonomy while preserving state fiscal powers.

5. PROTECTION OF STATES’ INTEREST

[!summary] Core Principle Certain financial bills can be introduced in Parliament only on President’s recommendation to safeguard state interests.

📋 FOUR CATEGORIES OF BILLS (Art. 117)

1️⃣ Tax/Duty Affecting States

  • Bills imposing or varying any tax or duty in which states are interested

2️⃣ Agricultural Income Definition

  • Bills varying meaning of ‘agricultural income’ as defined for Income Tax enactments

3️⃣ Distribution Principles

  • Bills affecting principles on which moneys are or may be distributable to states

4️⃣ Surcharge for Centre

  • Bills imposing surcharge on any specified tax/duty for Centre’s purpose

6. BORROWING POWERS: CENTRE vs STATES

📊 BORROWING POWERS: COMPARISON TABLE

Parameter 1️⃣ Centre (Art. 292) 2️⃣ State (Art. 293)
Territory Within or outside India Within India ONLY
Security Consolidated Fund of India (CFI) Consolidated Fund of State
Guarantees Can give guarantees Can give guarantees
Limit fixed by Parliament State Legislature

[!note] Key Difference External borrowing: Centre ✅ | State ❌

3️⃣ CENTRE-TO-STATE LENDING

  • Centre can make loans to any state
  • Centre can give guarantees for state loans

4️⃣ RESTRICTION ON STATES ⚠️

State CANNOT raise loan without Centre’s consent if:

  • Any part of Centre-made loan is still outstanding, OR
  • Centre has given guarantee for previous loan

[!danger] Federal Control Mechanism This gives Centre veto power over state borrowing when Centre exposure exists.

7. INTER-GOVERNMENTAL TAX IMMUNITIES

[!summary] Core Principle Rule of ‘immunity from mutual taxation’: Centre and States cannot tax each other’s property/income.

A) CENTRAL PROPERTY → STATE TAXATION EXEMPT

  • Covers every property with monetary value
  • Parliament can remove this immunity (empowered to lift ban)
  • #🚫

[!failure]- #🚫 Corporations/Companies → NOT immune (separate legal entity)

B) STATE PROPERTY/INCOME → CENTRAL TAXATION EXEMPT

  • State property & income exempt from Centre’s tax

[!failure]- #🚫 Critical Exceptions (NOT exempt):

  1. Local authorities within state → Taxable by Centre
  2. Corporations/Companies → Taxable by Centre (separate legal entity)

[!question]- Active Recall: Tax Immunities Q1: Can Parliament remove Central property’s immunity from State tax?

Click for Answers **A1:** Yes, Parliament empowered to remove ban

[!tip] Prelims Trap Immunity applies to government proper, not to instrumentalities like corporations, companies, or local bodies.

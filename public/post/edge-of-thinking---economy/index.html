3. FRBM Act, 2003 vs. Debt-to-GDP Fiscal Policy

Parameter FRBM Act, 2003 (Original Fiscal Deficit Target) Debt-to-GDP Ratio (The New Anchor)
Core Metric Fiscal Deficit: The gap between this year’s earning and spending. Total Outstanding Debt: The accumulated historical borrowing of the nation.
The Target Historically set at $3%$ of GDP annually. Targeted at $50%$ of GDP for the Centre by 2031 (Current: ~55.6%).
Nature of Target Flow Concept: Measures the speed at which new water (debt) is entering the tank this year. Stock Concept: Measures the total amount of water (debt) already accumulated in the tank.
Governance Approach Rigid & Short-term: The government must meet the target strictly by March 31st every year. Flexible & Long-term: The government follows a multi-year “glide path” over a decade.
Economic Stance Tends to be Pro-cyclical (forces the government to cut spending during a crisis to meet the rigid target). Allows for Counter-cyclical policy (gives room to spend during a crisis, as long as long-term debt is stable).

🧠 Why Shift? Economic-Survey

[!INFO] Core Concepts Based on N.K. Singh Committee & Fiscal Space [Rajkoshiya Avkash].

1. Pro-Cyclical Trap (FRBM Flaw) 📉

  • Crisis → GDP↓ → Tax↓ → To meet 3% → Spend↓ (e.g., stop highways)
  • Result: Cutting spend during recession worsens recession.

2. Denominator Effect (Debt-to-GDP) 📈

  • Formula: $$\frac{Total\ Debt}{Total\ GDP}$$
  • Logic: Grow GDP (denominator) faster than Debt (numerator).
  • Mechanism: Run higher deficit (4.5-5%) → Capex → GDP↑ → Ratio Falls.
  • Result: Acts as Shock Absorber (Counter-cyclical).

🏛️ Administrative Conclusion

2003 Context Today’s Context
Reckless borrowing history $4 Trillion Economy
Needed rigid discipline Volatile Geopolitics
Goal: Stop populism Goal: Agility + Solvency

==Verdict==: Debt-to-GDP ensures long-term solvency + tactical freedom to accelerate/brake economy.

3. The Vital “Financing” Data (Where is the 4.3% coming from?)

If the government is running a 4.3% Fiscal Deficit (₹16.8 Lakh Crore), how exactly is it arranging this massive amount of cash? This is called the Financing of the Fiscal Deficit.

  • Market Borrowings (G-Secs): Accounts for approximately 68% to 70% of the total borrowing. The government sells bonds to domestic banks and insurance companies.

  • Small Savings Fund (NSSF): Accounts for about 20% to 22%. This is the money common citizens deposit in Post Offices, Public Provident Fund (PPF), and Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana. The government heavily taps into these public savings to fund its deficit.

  • External Debt: Barely 2% to 3%. As discussed, India relies almost entirely on domestic money, insulating us from global currency crises.
